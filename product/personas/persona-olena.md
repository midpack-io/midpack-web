# Persona — Olena P., Head of Design at a growing fashion brand

## Role

Olena is the Head of Design at a 20–100-person fashion brand with regular collection releases — could be DTC, wholesale, retail-with-stores, or a mix; the sales channel doesn't define her day. She's 32–42, has 8–15 years in fashion, usually a design degree (LASALLE, Polimoda, Central Saint Martins, or a strong local equivalent), and worked her way up through a bigger brand or two before joining her current company 2–4 years ago. She reports to the founder/CEO and oversees a team of 3–8 — designers, often a tech designer or two, sometimes a product developer. She owns the creative output of every collection but also, by accident of organizational reality, owns the process that gets the collection from sketch to shippable. Nobody officially gave her that second job; it accreted because she was the one who noticed things falling through the cracks. Over the last year she's tried to push back — "this is operations, not design" — but the operations director has six other fires and the founder still asks her, not him, when a style is late.

## Typical week

- **Monday morning, 10am:** weekly product meeting with the founder, the operations director, production lead, and her tech designers. They walk through the collection calendar — SS26 fit reviews this week, FW26 design lock next week, the holiday capsule shipping Friday. Olena leads through the styles, calling out blockers. The founder makes 3–4 final calls; the rest she'll handle herself.
- **Tuesday + Wednesday:** the actual design work — but interrupted. She reviews her designers' boards in the morning, gives notes in the afternoon, and twice a week sits with the tech designer to align fit comments before they go to production. In between: Slack pings from the production lead asking if a fabric substitution is okay, a WhatsApp from the factory asking which sleeve length is correct on style 247, a Notion comment from the founder on a tech pack she hasn't seen yet.
- **Wednesday afternoon (the good window):** if the universe cooperates, she gets 2–3 hours of uninterrupted sketching for next season. Half the weeks this gets eaten by sample reviews.
- **Thursday afternoon (the high-stakes moment):** fit review on the FW26 first samples. 18 styles arrived from production this week. The room has the samples on dressforms, two designers, the tech designer, the operations director. They go through styles for two and a half hours. Olena makes fit calls live, the tech designer takes notes in her own notebook, somebody snaps phone photos for reference. By the end: a notebook page with shorthand only the tech designer understands, 60 phone photos in someone's camera roll, three follow-up Slack threads, and a verbal agreement that fit comments go to the factory "by Monday."
- **Friday:** the comments don't go by Monday. They go Tuesday — when the tech designer, between her own meetings, types them up. Two of Thursday's decisions get remembered wrong. One gets dropped entirely and surfaces three weeks later as a wrong sample.
- **Recurring artifact:** the collection master deck — a Figma/Keynote file Olena maintains for each season. It has every style, key fabrics, color story, delivery dates. The founder uses it for board meetings. The sales team uses it for showrooms. Nobody on production uses it because it's the wrong granularity. Olena updates it Sunday nights because there's no other quiet time.

## Measured on

- **Collection delivered on calendar** — late drops kill margins, retail relationships, e-com plans. The founder doesn't say "good job" when it's on time; she says nothing if it's late, which is worse.
- **Sample iteration count** — every extra round costs money and weeks. Olena watches this even when nobody asks. Three rounds per style is normal; four is a red flag; five is a crisis.
- **Creative coherence of the collection** — judged subjectively by the founder, by press, by the sales team's faces in the showroom. Olena's reputation rides on this and she knows it.
- **Team retention** — her tech designer quit a year ago because "the process was chaos." Olena promised herself it wouldn't happen again. She's been losing that promise slowly.
- **Founder confidence** — soft metric, but the most important one. The founder either trusts Olena to "handle it" or starts micromanaging. Olena lives for the former.

## Good at

- Reading a sketch and knowing immediately what won't translate to a tech pack.
- Holding the visual story of the whole collection together — what each piece does for the others, what's missing, what's redundant.
- Translating founder feedback ("this feels off") into specific design instructions her team can act on.
- Knowing which fit comment is critical and which is "the sample is fine."
- Calming the room when the production lead and the tech designer disagree — usually by making a decision that ends the argument, not by being right.

## What slows them down (ranked, most severe first)

1. **Fit review decisions die between Thursday and Monday.** The fit session produces decisions in the room. They live in a notebook, 60 phone photos, three Slack threads, and the tech designer's memory. By Monday two are remembered wrong and one is forgotten. The factory cuts the next sample with stale information. Olena finds out 3 weeks later when the wrong sample arrives. Persists because the review happens in physical space with samples and people, but there is no single artifact at the end that says "for style 247 at this stage, here are the 4 decisions and who approved them." The notebook is not that artifact. Slack is not that artifact. Nothing is.

2. **Tech pack versions slip out of sync between Olena, the tech designer, and the factory.** Tech designer sends v3 on WhatsApp Thursday. Olena approves v3 verbally on Friday. Factory's WhatsApp is on the production manager's phone, not the cutting room's; cutting room references "the latest tech pack" via email, where v2 was sent. Wrong sample arrives. Two weeks gone. Persists because tech packs are PDFs that travel by attachment, version is in the filename if anyone remembered to update it, and the source of truth depends on which app you happen to check first.

3. **The "is this approved?" question takes 15 minutes to answer every time.** Production lead asks: "Did Olena approve the green for style 247?" To answer: check Slack (was it discussed?), check email (was a doc shared?), check the Google Doc for color approvals (was it updated?), ask Olena directly (interrupting her work). Multiply by 40 styles per collection. Persists because approvals happen in different channels for different artifact types — fit in person, color in Google Doc, fabric on Slack, costing in email — and there's no unified "what's approved on this style at this stage" view.

4. **Onboarding a new team member takes 3 months because the process lives in heads.** When the new tech designer started last spring, she spent her first two months asking "but how do we usually...?" for everything. The answer was always "ask Olena" or "ask the previous tech designer" (who had left). Persists because the process is not encoded anywhere — it lives as habits, Slack history, and folder conventions. There's no system to onboard people into; there's a team to absorb into.

5. **The founder reviews tech packs out of order.** Founder has 20 minutes between meetings, opens whichever tech pack is most recent in Drive, leaves comments — sometimes on a v1 when v3 already addressed her concerns, sometimes on a style that's already at production. Tech designer spends an hour explaining why the comment doesn't apply anymore. Founder feels ignored. Persists because Drive has no concept of "current version at current stage"; it just shows files and folders. The founder is being thoughtful with her time and the tool is making her look careless.

6. **She's the bottleneck on creative approval and she can't delegate it.** Every fit call, every color story decision, every "does this style belong in the collection" call goes through her. On a busy week, 6–10 styles sit idle waiting for her 20-minute call. She tried delegating to a senior designer once; the senior designer made calls Olena disagreed with and Olena spent a week unwinding them. Never again. Persists because her judgment is the brand, and there's no version of the tool that says "Olena, here are the 5 decisions waiting on you, in priority order, do them now." Instead her decisions are scattered across requests in different channels and she handles them as they pop, not by priority.

## What they secretly want

- For the fit review to produce a real artifact by Friday morning — without anyone having to write a Monday-blocking summary email.
- One screen that shows the whole collection by stage — "in design, in tech pack, at factory, in fit review, approved for production" — that she can show the founder Monday morning without prep.
- Her tech designer to stop quitting — which means the process has to feel sane, not heroic.
- To not be asked "is this approved?" 30 times a week — the answer should be visible to whoever asks.
- The factory to always have the right version — without her or her tech designer policing which app it was sent on.
- To get her Wednesday afternoon sketching back — currently eaten by status questions she shouldn't be the source of truth for.
- To look in control to the founder — proactively, not reactively. "Style 247 is in third sample, addressed your two comments, here's where we are" instead of "let me find out and get back to you."

## Who they're NOT

- **Not a tech designer.** The tech designer owns measurements, BOM accuracy, factory communication on construction details. Olena owns creative direction and fit judgment. iterate.guru lets her see and approve the tech pack at the right stage, not author its specs.
- **Not an operations director.** The operations director (when one exists) owns calendars, vendor contracts, and shipping. Olena owns the creative process, which keeps getting confused with operations because nobody else is paying attention. She does not want to formally own ops; she wants ops not to fall on her by default.
- **Not a project manager.** PMs in fashion are rare and usually live in production, not design. Olena doesn't think in Gantt charts; she thinks in "the collection is or isn't coherent and is or isn't going to ship on time."
- **Not a Centric or FlexPLM buyer.** Those tools are for brands where a dedicated PLM admin spends three months configuring stage gates and approval matrices. Olena does not have three months, a PLM admin, or a procurement department. She has a credit card and a Tuesday afternoon.
- **Not the founder.** Even in brands where the founder still sketches, the founder lives in strategic decisions and external relationships. Olena lives in the production loop. The founder causes some of Olena's pain (by reviewing things out of order, by changing her mind on Thursday); Olena manages the pain.

## In their words

> "I made the call on the sleeve length. I made it in the room on Thursday. By Monday the factory's cutting it wrong and apparently that's my fault for not putting it in writing."

> "I have a notebook. The tech designer has a notebook. Production has a spreadsheet. The factory has WhatsApp. We are all writing down the same decisions in four places and somehow nobody has the right version."

> "If my tech designer quits because of this again I'm going to lose my mind."

> "The founder reviews tech packs out of order. She means well. The tool is the problem."

> "I don't need PLM. I've seen PLM. PLM is a three-month implementation project I do not have time for. I need the thing to work by Friday."

> "When somebody asks 'is the green approved?' the answer should be on a screen, not in my head."

> "I want to spend Wednesday afternoon sketching, not answering 'where's the v3 tech pack' for the fourth time this week."
