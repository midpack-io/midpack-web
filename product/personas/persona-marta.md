# Persona — Marta K., Tech Designer at a growing fashion brand

## Role

Marta is a tech designer at a 20–100-person fashion brand running 2–4 collections a year — the same kind of brand where someone like Olena P. runs design. She's 28–35, has 5–10 years in fashion, came up through a fashion-technology program (LCF pattern-cutting MA, Polimoda's tech-design track, or a strong local technical-college equivalent) and spent her first 3–4 years as an assistant tech designer at a bigger brand where there was a PLM admin and a system that, while heavy, at least existed. She joined her current company about 18 months ago — partly because the creative work was the kind she actually wanted to make producible, partly because she liked Olena in the interview. She reports to Olena. There is sometimes a second, more junior tech designer on the team; just as often, she's the only one. She owns the tech packs end-to-end: measurements, points-of-measure tables, construction notes, BOM, factory-facing communication on construction. She does not own the calendar, the creative direction, or commercial relationships with factories. She owns what happens between the sketch and the cutting room. Three months before she was hired, the previous tech designer quit — Olena described it in the interview as "the process got chaotic and we lost her." Marta has come to understand that "help me think about how we work" is a separate, unpaid second job that comes with the role, and that the brand cannot afford to lose another tech designer to the thing that took the last one.

## Typical week

- **Monday, 8am, before standup:** Marta opens WhatsApp. The factory is 4–6 hours ahead; there are 9 overnight messages across two threads. Three are real questions ("the cuff width on style 247 — is it 6cm or 6.5cm finished?"), four are status updates, two are voice notes she'll listen to on the walk in. By the time the 10am product meeting starts, she's already triaged her week.
- **Monday product meeting:** Marta is the second-most-asked person in the room after Olena. The production lead asks her, not Olena, whether style 247's PP sample is on track. The founder asks her whether the green lab dip got approved on Friday. She has the answers. Olena does not always realize Marta has the answers — sometimes Olena answers anyway.
- **Tuesday morning:** the real work, if it happens. Marta drafts tech packs from this week's locked sketches. This is what she was hired to do. She gets 90 minutes before the first interruption — a Slack from a designer asking which seam allowance Olena prefers on the new shirt, a WhatsApp from the factory asking which version of the costing sheet to quote against.
- **Wednesday morning:** sits with Olena to align fit comments before they go out to the factory. This is the meeting where two of Thursday's decisions get remembered differently — Marta's notebook says one thing, Olena's memory says another. Whichever of them is more confident in the moment usually wins.
- **Thursday afternoon, fit review:** Marta is the note-taker. 18 styles on dressforms, Olena calling fit, two designers, the operations director half-listening. Marta writes in her notebook with shorthand only she can decode, photographs critical seams on her phone, and tries to remember which decision was "do this" versus "let's see the next sample." By the end she has 60 photos, three notebook pages, two Slack threads that started during the meeting, and a verbal commitment that comments go out by Monday.
- **Friday:** should be comment-typing day. Usually isn't. Friday is fabric and color approvals she's also managing — the lab dips Olena needs to call before Tuesday, the linings that need to be ordered before next week. Comments slide to Monday morning.
- **Monday and Tuesday following:** types up Thursday's comments, double-checks with Olena (who is now also remembering Thursday slightly wrong), drafts the factory message, attaches the v3 tech pack PDF, sends. Two days after the decisions were made. The factory's cutting room is on a different email address than the production manager's WhatsApp; Marta keeps a private mental map of which channel each factory's cutting room actually reads.
- **Recurring artifact:** the master tech-pack folder per style — `Style 247 / Tech Pack / v3_final_FINAL.pdf`. Marta has a folder structure that works. Nobody else uses it. When a designer or the founder asks "where's the latest tech pack on 247?" she copy-pastes a link rather than explaining the folder; it's faster.

## Measured on

- **Tech-pack accuracy** — when the first PP sample comes back wrong, the question in the room is always "was the tech pack clear?" Marta watches this even when nobody asks.
- **Sample iteration count** — shared metric with Olena. If a style takes five rounds, it's a process failure; the process failure usually gets pinned somewhere, and Marta watches where.
- **Factory communication clarity** — how often a factory comes back with "we need clarification" rather than the right sample. Internal, unmeasured, but Marta knows the number.
- **Olena's trust** — does Olena check with her before making a call that affects production, or does she just decide and tell Marta after? The first version is a good week. The second version is a bad month.
- **"Don't get blamed when something breaks"** — implicit, never written down, the most exhausting one. When the wrong sample arrives three weeks after Thursday's fit review, the question is whose fault it is. Marta has a private archive of WhatsApp screenshots and email timestamps for exactly this reason.

## Good at

- Translating creative intent into producible spec — knowing what a factory will need to know before the factory asks.
- Catching construction issues at sketch stage — flagging "this collar can't be cut on the bias the way you've drawn it" before the tech pack gets written, saving a sample round.
- Reading factory pushback — knowing when "we can't do that" is a real constraint versus a negotiating position.
- Holding ground on fabric substitutions — saying "no, we approved 100% cotton" calmly to the production lead at 5pm Friday.
- File and folder hygiene nobody uses — she's the only person on the team with a consistent versioning scheme, and the team's response is to ask her where files are instead of learning the scheme.

## What slows them down (ranked, most severe first)

1. **Reconstructing Thursday's fit decisions on Tuesday.** Her notebook, Olena's notebook, the 60 phone photos, the three Slack threads — two days later, Marta is writing the factory comment matrix from memory and partial sources. She is 80% confident she has each decision right. The other 20% surfaces three weeks later as a wrong sample, and the question in the room is whether the tech pack was clear. The fit review produced decisions in physical space with samples and people, and there is no artifact at the end that says "for style 247 at fit-review stage, these four decisions were made by these people at this time."

2. **Being the person everyone asks "did Olena approve this?"** The factory asks her at 7am. The production lead asks her at lunch. The founder asks her over Slack at 9pm. To answer she has to check Slack (was it discussed?), check email (was a doc shared?), check the lab-dip Google Doc, and sometimes interrupt Olena. The 15 minutes Olena spends answering "is this approved?" — Marta spends it too, in parallel, for the same answer. When she's wrong, she's blamed; when she's right, nobody notices.

3. **Which version is the factory actually cutting from.** She sends v3 on WhatsApp to the production manager Thursday. The cutting room references "the latest tech pack" off an email where v2 was attached. Nobody wrote down which version was confirmed at which stage. Two weeks later: wrong sample, three weeks lost, $500–800 in extra sample cost. Marta sends v3 again with "this is the current one" — and waits for the next acknowledgement she can't verify.

4. **The founder comments on stale versions.** The founder has 20 minutes between meetings, opens whichever tech pack is most recent in her Drive view (not the one currently at the relevant stage), leaves comments — half of them on v1 when v3 has addressed them. Marta spends an hour writing "this was addressed in v3, the change is on the construction page line 4." It feels like defending herself. The founder is being thoughtful with her time; the tools are making her look careless and making Marta look slow to respond.

5. **No time for the technical work she was hired for.** Patternmaking liaison, construction problem-solving, grading reviews, lining-attachment alternatives, factory technical capabilities — this is the work that makes her good at her job and that she wanted to do. Instead she's a version-control human and a question-routing API. The skills she came in with are atrophying. She notices.

6. **Her work is invisible until something breaks.** Across the SS26 collection she sent 47 tech packs, 41 of which went through with two or fewer revisions. There is no surface that shows that. There is only the conversation in the room when the 47th one comes back wrong. "The process feels chaotic" lands at her feet by default, because she's the closest person to where chaos was supposed to be prevented. Olena's tech designer quit a year ago because of exactly this dynamic; Marta is aware of the precedent.

## What they secretly want

- One source of truth for "the current tech pack at the current stage" — so she stops being the live index everyone queries.
- The founder commenting on the right version — so the hour she spends translating stale comments goes away.
- Fit-review decisions captured as an artifact during or right after the review — so Tuesday isn't reconstruction day.
- A timestamp that says "Olena approved v3 of style 247 on Friday at 2:14pm" — so when the factory says they didn't get it or the room debates what was decided, the record exists outside her notebook and her memory.
- Visibility on the work she's doing right — so when 41 of 47 tech packs ship clean, somebody sees that before the 47th one breaks.
- To do technical work again — the actual construction-and-grading work, not version policing.
- To not quit. The last tech designer at this company quit because of this. Marta does not want to be the second.

## Who they're NOT

- **Not a designer.** Marta does not decide what a collection looks like, whether a style belongs, or what the silhouette should be. Olena owns those calls. Marta owns "given this sketch, here's exactly how it gets cut, in what fabric, with what construction, at what measurements." Midpack gives her the workflow around the tech pack — not authorship of the design.
- **Not a project manager.** She does not own the collection calendar, the production schedule, or vendor deadlines. The operations director (when one exists) and Olena own those. Marta is accountable for her own outputs hitting their stage gates — not for the collection shipping.
- **Not a vendor liaison.** The commercial relationship with each factory — pricing, capacity allocation, exclusivity, payment terms — is Olena's, the founder's, and ops's. Marta is the technical contact within those relationships, not the relationship owner. When a factory tries to renegotiate price on a WhatsApp, she escalates rather than answers.
- **Not a pattern maker.** Patternmaking lives at the factory in most setups. Marta specs the points of measure, the construction details, and the grading rules; the factory drafts the actual pattern. Marta reviews the first cut and flags what's off — she doesn't draw the pattern herself.
- **Not a PLM admin.** Marta has used PLM at a bigger brand. She knows the surface — the stage-gate matrices, the BOM modules, the approval workflows. She also knows what it cost: three months of setup, a dedicated admin, training cycles for every new hire. She is not asking for PLM. She is asking for the thing PLM was supposed to be before it grew into an implementation project. The absence of any system at her current company is worse than PLM was — but the answer is not to bring PLM back.

## In their words

> "Olena made the call in the fit room on Thursday. By Tuesday she remembers it one way and I remember it another. Whichever of us has more political capital that week wins. There is no neutral record."

> "I have a notebook. Olena has a notebook. The factory has WhatsApp. The cutting room has an email I'm not on. We are all writing down the same decisions in four places and somehow nobody has the right version when the sample is being cut."

> "The founder is the worst comment-leaver in the building, and she means well. She opens whatever tech pack is most recent in her Drive — never the one at the stage we're actually working on — and writes notes for v1 when v3 fixed that page two weeks ago. I spend an hour every Friday writing 'this was addressed.'"

> "I want to be doing technical work. Construction. Grading. Lining attachment. The reason I have this degree. Instead I am a human version-control system and a routing layer for the question 'did Olena approve this?'"

> "I've used PLM. It is not what we need. But what we have now is worse than PLM was — and I'm not going to spend three months setting up PLM to find that out twice."

> "I'm not going to be the second tech designer who quits this company because of the process. I don't say that out loud. But it's the thing I keep checking against."
