# Logo Brief: Midpack

## Context for the AI Generator / Designer

Midpack is a PLM-like product for small and mid-sized fashion brands. We sit between the indie spreadsheet-and-Slack chaos and the $120k/year enterprise PLMs (Centric, Kōbō). Our pricing model is per-bundle, not per-seat — brands pay for the volume of work in development, not for the number of seats around the table.

The product is used daily by fashion brand owners, operations leads, and designers — people who think in terms of collections, styles, suppliers, fittings, and production cycles. They appreciate restraint over flash. They've seen enough overdesigned SaaS logos to last them a lifetime.

**Bottom line:** the logo should feel like a tool that belongs in a serious studio, not a marketing campaign.

---

## What the name means

Two layers worth knowing:

- **Mid** — we serve the middle of the brand-size distribution. Not enterprise, not hobbyist.
- **Pack** — in fashion, a *pack* is a coordinated set of styles being developed together. It's also the bundle our pricing is anchored on. Brands buy capacity in packs, not seats.

A logo that quietly carries either meaning is welcome. A logo that screams either meaning is not.

---

## Brand attributes

The logo should read as:

- **Precise** — like a tool with measured tolerances
- **Calm** — not loud, not animated, not "fun"
- **Expert** — confident enough not to over-explain itself
- **Modular** — feels like it could be part of a system, not a one-off illustration
- **Quiet sophistication** — closer to an architecture firm than a fashion week brand

It should *not* read as:

- Trendy startup ("vibey" gradients, organic blobs, abstract waves)
- Fashion cliché (needles, threads, hangers, mannequins, scissors, dresses)
- Enterprise software (corporate blue, cube-like marks, "trust" symbolism)
- AI tool (sparkles, stars, neural-network nodes)

---

## Logo system

Deliver a small system, not a single image. At minimum:

1. **Primary wordmark** — "Midpack" set in a considered typeface. This is the main asset and the one used most often.
2. **Lockup variant** — wordmark + optional symbol/mark, for cases where a mark alone is needed (favicon, app icon, profile photo).
3. **Mark-only** — a standalone glyph derived from the wordmark or from a single letter (the *M*, or the *M*+*p* relationship). Should hold up at 16×16 px (favicon) and 1024×1024 px (app store icon).

Optional but appreciated: a **monogram variant** for stamp-like use (decks, footers, watermarks).

---

## Typography direction

- Sans-serif. Geometric or humanist, both are on the table. Examples of *territory* (not exact references): GT America, Söhne, Inter, Neue Haas Grotesk, Founders Grotesk, Aktiv Grotesk.
- Avoid: condensed display faces, novelty fonts, anything decorative, anything that screams "AI-generated logo."
- Slight optical adjustments are welcome (custom kerning, a subtly modified letterform). A custom *p* descender or a tied *id* ligature would be in-character.
- Weight: regular to medium. Not thin (too fragile), not heavy (too aggressive).

---

## Color

**No specific colors are required.** The logo must work in pure monochrome — black on white, white on black. Any color expression beyond that is a system layer added later, not part of the logo's identity. Show the logo in mono first.

If you want to propose a single accent color as part of the system, it should be:
- Restrained (not saturated, not neon)
- Defensible at small sizes
- Not blue. Not the standard "tech startup blue."

---

## Form considerations

- The wordmark should hold its shape across sizes from 14 px tall (UI nav) to a printed business card to a 4-meter conference banner.
- No 3D, no shadows, no gradients, no bevels.
- No animations, no isometric perspectives.
- The mark should be derivable from a clear geometric logic — explainable in one sentence ("it's the *M* with the inner counter shaped like a pack," or similar). If you can't explain it in one sentence, it's too clever.
- White-space integrity matters: the mark should feel intentional even when surrounded by lots of empty space, which is how it'll mostly be used.

---

## Where it will appear

So the candidate can pressure-test legibility across contexts:

- Top-left navigation of a web app (small, often paired with secondary nav)
- Mobile app icon (square, must survive at favicon and home-screen sizes)
- Sales decks (footer, slide corner)
- PDF documents (cover and footer)
- Email signature
- Browser tab favicon (16×16)
- Stickers and merch (later, but worth being printable)

---

## Deliverables

- Primary wordmark in SVG, PNG (1× / 2× / 3×), and PDF
- Mark-only / lockup variants in the same set
- Light-on-dark and dark-on-light versions
- Clear-space rules (minimum padding around the mark, expressed in units of the mark's own height)
- Minimum-size guidance (the smallest legible rendering)
- A short usage page: do/don't examples (no stretching, no recoloring outside the system, no rotation, no enclosing in shapes)

---

## What to submit per concept

For each direction proposed, show:

1. The wordmark at large size, isolated, on white
2. The mark-only version at 24 px, 64 px, and 256 px to prove it scales
3. One real-world mock: top-left of a web app navigation
4. One real-world mock: a favicon in a browser tab
5. One sentence describing the conceptual logic of the mark

---

## Anti-checklist (what disqualifies a concept)

- Includes a needle, thread, hanger, mannequin, dress, fabric pattern, button, zipper, or scissors
- Uses a sparkle, star, or any "AI" iconography
- Renders illegibly at favicon size
- Relies on color to communicate identity
- Uses more than two distinct geometric elements in the mark
- Includes a tagline baked into the lockup
- Looks like any of: Notion, Linear, Vercel, Stripe, Figma, Airtable, Asana (all admirable, all already taken)

---

## Concept, not a final spec

This brief describes the territory and the constraints, not the answer. Surprise me within these bounds. If a constraint here is the thing standing between you and the right answer, push back with reasoning — concepts that argue for themselves are more useful than concepts that just comply.
